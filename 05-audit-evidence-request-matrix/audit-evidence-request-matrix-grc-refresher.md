# Audit Evidence Request Matrix – GRC Refresher Notes

## What it is

An audit evidence request matrix is a structured tracker used to define what evidence is needed to support control validation during an audit, assessment, or compliance review.

It helps answer:
- what control is being reviewed
- what evidence is needed
- who owns the evidence
- how often the evidence should be reviewed
- what the due date is
- whether the evidence has been provided

In GRC terms, an audit evidence request matrix is a practical audit support artifact that connects:
- control validation
- evidence management
- accountability
- review readiness
- compliance documentation

## Why it matters

Audits often fail to run efficiently because organizations do not clearly define:
- what evidence is needed
- who should provide it
- when it is due
- whether it is complete
- whether it actually supports the control

Without a structured matrix, audits become chaotic:
- evidence requests are vague
- ownership is unclear
- deadlines slip
- incomplete evidence gets submitted
- reviewers waste time chasing documents

An audit evidence request matrix forces discipline.

That is why it matters in:
- internal audits
- compliance reviews
- control testing
- audit preparation
- evidence collection planning

## The logic of the main columns

### Control ID
A unique identifier for the control being reviewed.

Why it matters:
- supports traceability
- allows consistent reporting
- makes it easier to reference controls during review

Professional explanation:
> The Control ID provides a unique reference point for each control being tested or reviewed, supporting traceability and structured audit communication.

### Control Area
This groups the control into a subject area.

Examples:
- access control
- logging and monitoring
- change management
- vendor management
- data protection

Why it matters:
- helps organize reviews
- supports scoping and reporting
- reveals concentration of issues by control domain

Professional explanation:
> The Control Area groups related controls into review domains, which supports scoping, reporting, and audit organization.

### Control Objective
This explains what the control is intended to achieve.

Examples:
- restrict access based on business need
- ensure production changes are approved and tested
- protect customer and payment-related data

Professional explanation:
> The Control Objective defines the intended outcome of the control and clarifies what the evidence must ultimately support.

### Required Evidence
This states what documentation or artifact is needed to support the control.

Examples:
- access review reports
- approval records
- role matrix
- SIEM screenshots
- change tickets
- retention schedules
- encryption settings

Professional explanation:
> Required Evidence identifies the specific documents, reports, records, or system outputs needed to demonstrate that the control is operating as expected.

### Evidence Owner
This identifies the person or function responsible for providing the evidence.

Examples:
- IAM Manager
- Security Operations Lead
- IT Operations Manager
- Vendor Management Lead
- Data Protection Officer

Professional explanation:
> The Evidence Owner is the accountable party responsible for gathering and submitting the evidence needed for review.

### Review Frequency
This defines how often the evidence should be reviewed or refreshed.

Typical values:
- Monthly
- Quarterly
- Semi-Annual
- Annual

Why it matters:
Different controls require different review cadences depending on risk and control type.

Professional explanation:
> Review Frequency defines how often evidence should be examined or refreshed to support control oversight and audit readiness.

### Evidence Status
This tracks the current state of the request.

Typical values:
- Requested
- In Progress
- Open
- Received
- Closed

Professional explanation:
> Evidence Status provides visibility into whether requested support has been submitted, is still pending, or remains incomplete.

### Reviewer
This identifies the person or function evaluating the evidence.

Examples:
- Compliance Analyst
- Internal Auditor
- Risk Analyst
- Privacy Compliance Lead

Professional explanation:
> The Reviewer is the individual or function responsible for assessing whether the submitted evidence adequately supports the control objective.

### Due Date
This defines when the evidence should be submitted or reviewed by.

Why it matters:
- prevents open-ended follow-up
- improves accountability
- keeps review activity on schedule

Professional explanation:
> The Due Date establishes the expected timing for evidence submission or review, supporting disciplined audit execution.

### Notes
This provides supporting context.

Examples:
- scope limitations
- review focus
- systems in scope
- reminders about privileged access or PCI relevance

Professional explanation:
> The Notes field captures important context that improves the quality, focus, or relevance of the evidence review.

## Helpful distinctions

### Control vs evidence
- The control is the safeguard or process
- The evidence is the proof that the control exists and is operating

Example:
- Control: quarterly access review
- Evidence: access review report, approvals, role matrix, sign-off records

### Evidence requested vs evidence sufficient
Just because evidence is submitted does not mean it is good enough.

Evidence may still be:
- incomplete
- outdated
- irrelevant
- inconsistent
- not clearly tied to the control objective

### Audit readiness vs audit success
Being organized for evidence collection improves readiness.
It does not guarantee the control will pass review.
