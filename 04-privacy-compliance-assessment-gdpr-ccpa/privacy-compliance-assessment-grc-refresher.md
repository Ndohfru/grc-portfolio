# Privacy Compliance Assessment – GRC Refresher Notes

## What it is

A privacy compliance assessment is a structured review of how an organization collects, uses, stores, shares, and retains personal information against applicable privacy requirements.

It helps answer:
- what privacy obligations apply
- what personal data is being processed
- how the current process operates
- where privacy gaps exist
- what risk those gaps create
- what remediation should happen next

In GRC terms, a privacy compliance assessment is a regulatory and governance artifact that connects:
- privacy obligations
- data handling practices
- documentation quality
- control awareness
- remediation planning
- accountability

## Why it matters

Organizations often process personal data across:
- customer onboarding
- booking and payment flows
- support channels
- analytics tools
- vendor platforms
- retention and deletion processes

Without structured review, they tend to assume:
- the privacy notice is enough
- consent and lawful basis are already covered
- retention practices are acceptable
- consumer rights requests can be handled if they appear

That is weak privacy governance.

A privacy compliance assessment forces structured review of:
- legal and regulatory expectations
- operational practice
- ownership
- evidence of control
- open privacy gaps

That is why privacy assessments matter in:
- GDPR and CCPA readiness
- privacy program development
- internal control reviews
- regulatory response preparedness
- data governance improvement

## What this project proves

The SkyBridge privacy compliance assessment proves that you can:
- identify privacy obligations in a business scenario
- compare current state against GDPR and CCPA expectations
- document privacy gaps clearly
- connect those gaps to business and regulatory risk
- recommend remediation actions

That is real privacy and compliance thinking.

## The logic of the main columns

### Requirement ID
A unique identifier for each privacy requirement being assessed.

Why it matters:
- supports traceability
- helps with reporting and follow-up
- prevents confusion during review

Professional explanation:
> The Requirement ID provides a reference point for each privacy obligation being assessed, which supports tracking, remediation, and governance reporting.

### Regulation
This identifies the applicable privacy regulation or standard.

Examples:
- GDPR
- CCPA
- GDPR / CCPA

Why it matters:
Different requirements come from different legal obligations.

Professional explanation:
> The Regulation column anchors each row to the relevant privacy obligation so the assessment remains tied to actual regulatory expectations.

### Privacy Requirement
This identifies the actual privacy obligation being reviewed.

Examples:
- lawful basis for processing
- data subject rights handling
- notice at collection
- right to opt out
- data retention and minimization

Professional explanation:
> The Privacy Requirement column defines the obligation that the organization is expected to meet under the applicable privacy regime.

### Current State
This describes what currently exists in the environment.

It should answer:
- how the process currently works
- whether there is a documented process
- whether implementation appears consistent
- whether evidence seems available

Professional explanation:
> Current State documents how the organization presently handles the privacy-related activity in practice, including the maturity and consistency of current processes.

### Gap Identified
This states what is missing, weak, inconsistent, or undocumented.

This is one of the most important columns.

A privacy gap is the specific difference between:
- what the privacy requirement expects
- and what the organization currently has in place

Professional explanation:
> The Gap Identified column documents the specific shortfall between the regulatory requirement and the current operating reality.

### Risk Impact
This explains why the gap matters.

Possible impacts:
- regulatory non-compliance
- privacy complaints
- consumer trust erosion
- over-retention of personal data
- delayed response to rights requests
- increased exposure in the event of an incident

Professional explanation:
> Risk Impact translates the privacy gap into business, regulatory, and reputational consequences that matter to management and stakeholders.

### Priority
This reflects urgency.

Typical values:
- High
- Medium
- Low

Priority is influenced by:
- severity of the privacy gap
- sensitivity of the data
- regulatory exposure
- potential customer harm
- operational importance

Professional explanation:
> Priority helps determine which privacy gaps need immediate remediation and which can be addressed through scheduled corrective action.

### Recommended Action
This states what should happen next.

Examples:
- document lawful basis
- create formal rights request workflow
- add notice at collection
- define opt-out mechanism
- establish retention schedule

Professional explanation:
> Recommended Action defines the remediation step needed to close the privacy gap or reduce the related compliance and governance exposure.

### Owner
This identifies the accountable person or function.

Examples:
- Data Protection Officer
- Privacy Compliance Lead
- Records Management Lead
- Customer Privacy Manager
- E-Commerce Platform Owner

Professional explanation:
> The Owner is the accountable function responsible for coordinating remediation, documentation, or process improvement for the privacy issue.

### Target Date
This gives the remediation timeline.

Why it matters:
- prevents open-ended findings
- supports accountability
- allows review meetings to track progress

Professional explanation:
> The Target Date establishes when remediation is expected and helps convert privacy findings into accountable action.

### Status
This shows the current condition of the gap or remediation effort.

Typical values:
- Open
- In Progress
- Closed
- Under Review

Professional explanation:
> Status provides visibility into whether the privacy gap remains unresolved, is actively being remediated, or has been closed.

## Helpful distinctions

### Privacy requirement vs privacy control
- A privacy requirement is the obligation
- A privacy control is the mechanism used to meet that obligation

Example:
- Requirement: respond to data subject access requests
- Control: documented intake, validation, response, and tracking workflow

### Gap vs remediation action
- Gap = the shortfall
- Remediation action = the planned response

Example:
- Gap: no formal rights request workflow
- Remediation: create intake, validation, escalation, and response procedure

### Compliance readiness vs legal certainty
A privacy compliance assessment helps evaluate readiness and exposure.
It does not by itself establish legal certainty or regulatory approval.
