# Third-Party / Vendor Risk Assessment – GRC Refresher Notes

## What it is

A third-party or vendor risk assessment is a structured review of an external service provider to determine the level of risk the vendor introduces to the organization.

It helps answer:
- what service the vendor provides
- what type of data or system access the vendor has
- how critical the vendor is to operations
- what risks the vendor introduces
- what controls already exist
- what residual risk remains
- what remediation or oversight is needed

In GRC terms, a vendor risk assessment is a core artifact that connects:
- third-party oversight
- compliance monitoring
- operational dependency
- control review
- documentation and accountability

## Why it matters

Organizations rarely operate alone.

They depend on vendors for:
- cloud hosting
- payment processing
- analytics
- messaging
- identity verification
- software support
- infrastructure and managed services

That means third parties can introduce:
- security risk
- privacy risk
- compliance risk
- operational resilience risk
- concentration risk
- contractual risk

Without structured vendor risk review, organizations often assume:
- the vendor is secure
- the contract is enough
- the service works, so the risk is acceptable

That is weak governance.

A vendor risk assessment forces structured review.

## The logic of the main columns

### Vendor ID
A unique identifier for each vendor record.

Why it matters:
- makes the vendor traceable
- supports reporting and reference
- reduces confusion when multiple vendors are in scope

Professional explanation:
> The Vendor ID provides a consistent reference point for tracking vendor-related risk across review and reporting activities.

### Vendor Name
The legal or operating name of the vendor.

Professional explanation:
> The Vendor Name identifies the third party being assessed and anchors the record to a specific service relationship.

### Service Provided
This describes what the vendor actually does.

Examples:
- payment gateway
- cloud hosting
- email notification service
- analytics platform
- identity verification

Why it matters:
You cannot assess vendor risk correctly if you do not understand the service.

Professional explanation:
> The Service Provided column clarifies the business function supported by the vendor and helps determine the dependency and risk profile.

### Data / Access Type
This identifies what the vendor can access or handle.

Examples:
- cardholder data
- personal data
- customer contact data
- system hosting
- administrative access
- aggregated analytics data

Professional explanation:
> Data / Access Type defines the level of exposure created by the vendor relationship, including data sensitivity and system access implications.

### Criticality
This measures how important the vendor is to the business.

Typical values:
- High
- Medium
- Low

Criticality is influenced by:
- whether the service is operationally essential
- whether the business can function without it
- whether outages would create major disruption

Professional explanation:
> Criticality reflects how essential the vendor is to business operations and whether disruption would materially impact service delivery.

### Inherent Risk
This is the level of vendor-related risk before considering mitigating controls.

Examples of factors that drive inherent risk:
- sensitive data access
- external connectivity
- administrative privileges
- business dependency
- regulatory implications

Professional explanation:
> Inherent Risk represents the raw level of exposure created by the vendor relationship before considering controls, contractual safeguards, or oversight.

### Key Risk Factors
This explains why the vendor is risky.

Examples:
- handles payment data
- privileged access
- cloud dependency
- privacy exposure
- API integration
- service outage risk

Professional explanation:
> Key Risk Factors explain the primary drivers behind the vendor’s risk profile and provide context for scoring and treatment planning.

### Existing Controls
These are the safeguards already in place.

Examples:
- contract clauses
- due diligence review
- segmentation
- encryption requirements
- IAM restrictions
- SLA review
- monitoring

Professional explanation:
> Existing Controls represent current contractual, technical, or governance measures intended to reduce the vendor-related risk.

### Control Effectiveness
This assesses how well those controls are working.

Typical values:
- Effective
- Partially Effective
- Ineffective

This matters because many organizations have vendor controls on paper that are not consistently validated.

Professional explanation:
> Control Effectiveness reflects whether existing oversight and safeguards are operating well enough to meaningfully reduce vendor exposure.

### Residual Risk
This is the remaining risk after existing controls are considered.

Professional explanation:
> Residual Risk reflects the level of third-party exposure that remains after accounting for current controls and their effectiveness.

### Vendor Owner
This identifies the internal business owner responsible for the vendor relationship.

This is important.

The owner is usually not the vendor itself.
It is the internal accountable stakeholder.

Professional explanation:
> The Vendor Owner is the internal accountable party responsible for managing the vendor relationship, coordinating review activities, and escalating risk where needed.

### Review Frequency
This defines how often the vendor should be reassessed.

Typical values:
- Quarterly
- Semi-Annual
- Annual

Why it matters:
Higher-risk vendors should not be reviewed at the same cadence as low-risk vendors.

Professional explanation:
> Review Frequency establishes the oversight cadence based on vendor criticality, exposure, and residual risk.

### Remediation Actions
This states what needs to happen next to reduce vendor-related exposure.

Examples:
- validate logging controls
- strengthen privacy clauses
- review privileged access
- document incident notification requirements
- assess resilience obligations

Professional explanation:
> Remediation Actions define the next steps required to reduce unresolved third-party risk or strengthen vendor oversight.

### Status
This tracks the current state of the vendor risk issue or review.

Typical values:
- Open
- In Progress
- Closed

Professional explanation:
> Status provides visibility into whether vendor-related issues remain unresolved, are being addressed, or have been closed.

## Helpful distinctions

### Vendor criticality vs vendor risk
A vendor can be highly critical and also high risk, but they are not the same thing.

- Criticality = how important the service is to the business
- Risk = how much exposure the vendor introduces

### Inherent risk vs residual risk
- Inherent risk = exposure before controls
- Residual risk = exposure after controls are considered

### Due diligence vs ongoing monitoring
- Due diligence = what you assess before or during onboarding
- Ongoing monitoring = how you continue reviewing the vendor over time

A mature vendor risk program needs both.
