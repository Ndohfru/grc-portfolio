# Enterprise Risk Register – GRC Refresher Notes

## What it is

An enterprise risk register is a structured record of an organization’s important risks.

It helps answer:
- what could go wrong
- how serious it is
- what controls already exist
- who owns the risk
- what still needs to be done

In GRC terms, it is one of the most useful foundational artifacts because it connects:
- governance
- risk assessment
- control thinking
- accountability
- remediation

## Why it matters

A company without a risk register is often operating on memory, assumptions, and scattered conversations.

A risk register forces discipline.

It turns vague concerns like:
- “vendor risk is a problem”
- “compliance could fail”
- “our systems may be exposed”

into structured decision-making.

That is why risk registers matter in:
- audit preparation
- compliance programs
- management reporting
- remediation tracking
- risk review meetings

## What this project proves

The SkyBridge register proves that you can:
- identify business and technology risks
- categorize risks clearly
- think in terms of impact and likelihood
- connect risks to controls
- assign ownership
- recommend treatment actions

That is real GRC thinking.

## The logic of the main columns

### Risk ID
A unique identifier for each risk.

Why it matters:
- makes the risk traceable
- prevents confusion
- supports reporting and reference

Professional explanation:
> The Risk ID gives each risk a unique reference point for tracking, escalation, and reporting.

### Risk Category
This groups the risk into a type, such as:
- compliance
- cybersecurity
- third-party
- operational
- privacy
- governance

Why it matters:
- helps with reporting
- shows concentration areas
- reveals patterns across the environment

Professional explanation:
> Risk categories help segment exposure areas so management can see where risk is concentrated.

### Risk Description
This is the actual statement of the risk.

A strong risk statement usually says:
- what could happen
- why it matters
- what could be affected

Weak example:
> Poor security

Strong example:
> Inadequate access controls over payment systems could result in unauthorized access to sensitive data and regulatory non-compliance.

Professional explanation:
> The risk description defines the exposure clearly enough to support scoring, ownership, and treatment planning.

### Likelihood
How likely the risk is to happen.

Usually rated:
- Low
- Medium
- High

Why it matters:
Not every risk is equally probable.

Professional explanation:
> Likelihood reflects the probability of occurrence based on current conditions, exposure, and control maturity.

### Impact
How severe the consequence would be if the risk happens.

Could affect:
- operations
- compliance
- reputation
- finance
- data protection
- customer trust

Professional explanation:
> Impact reflects the business consequence if the risk materializes, including operational, regulatory, financial, or reputational harm.

### Inherent Risk
This is the risk level before considering controls.

A lot of beginners confuse inherent risk with residual risk.

Inherent risk asks:
> If no meaningful controls existed, how serious would this risk be?

Professional explanation:
> Inherent risk represents the raw level of exposure before accounting for existing controls or mitigation measures.

### Existing Controls
These are the safeguards already in place.

Examples:
- MFA
- access reviews
- vendor contracts
- encryption
- training
- monitoring
- policy requirements

Professional explanation:
> Existing controls represent current preventive, detective, or corrective measures that reduce likelihood or impact.

### Control Effectiveness
This assesses how well those controls are working.

Typical values:
- Effective
- Partially Effective
- Ineffective

This matters because many organizations have controls on paper that are weak in practice.

Professional explanation:
> Control effectiveness reflects whether current controls are operating as intended and meaningfully reducing exposure.

### Residual Risk
This is the remaining risk after existing controls are considered.

This is one of the most important columns in the whole register.

Professional explanation:
> Residual risk reflects the level of exposure that remains after considering current controls and their effectiveness.

### Risk Owner
This is the person or function accountable for the risk.

Not necessarily the person who caused it.
Not necessarily the person who implements every control.

It is the accountable owner.

Professional explanation:
> The risk owner is the accountable party responsible for monitoring, escalating, and coordinating treatment of the risk.

### Treatment Plan / Recommended Action
This is what should happen next.

Common treatment options:
- mitigate
- transfer
- accept
- avoid

In practice, this becomes actions like:
- implement MFA
- strengthen vendor due diligence
- update policy
- perform access reviews
- reduce retention period

Professional explanation:
> The treatment plan documents the action required to reduce, transfer, accept, or otherwise manage the identified risk.

### Status
This shows whether the risk action is:
- Open
- In Progress
- Closed
- Accepted
- Under Review

Professional explanation:
> Status helps track treatment progress and supports visibility into unresolved or actively managed risks.

## How to explain the register in an interview

Use something like this:

> I created an enterprise risk register using a fictional airline environment to practice structured risk identification and treatment in a GRC context. The register covered areas such as compliance, cybersecurity, third-party, operational, governance, privacy, and business continuity risk. I used fields for likelihood, impact, inherent risk, existing controls, control effectiveness, residual risk, ownership, and treatment actions to show how risk can be documented and tracked in a defensible way. The project helped me strengthen my understanding of how risks are translated into accountable action within a regulated environment.

That answer is strong because it is:
- honest
- practical
- framework-friendly
- not inflated

## The deeper lesson

This risk register is not just a spreadsheet.

It is a model of how GRC thinks:
- identify exposure
- evaluate seriousness
- examine controls
- determine remaining risk
- assign ownership
- drive action

That is the mindset you are trying to build.

## The mistake to avoid

Do not describe the register like this:
> I made a spreadsheet of risks.

That sounds administrative.

Describe it like this:
> I built a structured risk register to document, assess, and track organizational risks, control context, residual exposure, ownership, and treatment actions in a regulated business scenario.

That sounds like GRC.

## Suggested next study topic

The next high-value topic to study from this project is:
- inherent risk vs residual risk vs control effectiveness

That is where many beginners remain weak.
